How to choose a DMC — 10 questions to ask before you commit.

Every DMC's website says "local experts" and "tailor-made". The differences show up when you ask the right questions. Here are the ten we'd ask — including the ones we'd want to be asked ourselves.

Choosing a destination management company is choosing who you'll trust with your group, your budget and your reputation in a country you may not know. (If you're still working out whether you need one at all, start with What is a DMC?.) The good news: a few direct questions separate a dependable local partner from a reseller with a nice website.

1. Are you genuinely based in the destination?

Ask where the team that will plan your programme physically sits. Plenty of "DMCs" cover a destination from an office two countries away, reselling suppliers they've never visited. You want people who live where your group is going — the whole value of a DMC flows from that.

2. How long have you operated there?

Supplier relationships are a DMC's real inventory, and they take years to build. A firm that has weathered decades in a market has the relationships — and has survived enough crises to handle yours.

3. Who exactly will I be working with?

One named contact who owns your account from brief to delivery, or a rotating cast and a shared inbox? Ask for the name before you sign, not after.

4. What happens outside office hours?

Groups travel on weekends and flights land at midnight. Ask for the out-of-hours arrangement in writing — a real emergency line, not a voicemail. If the answer is vague, so is the support.

5. Do you own the whole chain — research, planning, booking, delivery?

Some firms only broker: they'll book what you specify but won't research alternatives, negotiate rates or stand next to the programme while it runs. A full-service DMC takes the brief and returns a finished programme.

6. Can you show me a programme like mine?

Not a glossy brochure — a concrete example: a group of similar size, similar goal, same destination. How was it structured? What went wrong and how was it handled? The second question is the revealing one.

7. How is your pricing built?

You're looking for an itemised, tailor-made proposal with costs visible before you commit — and clear payment and cancellation terms that follow the actual supplier conditions. Be wary of quotes that are suspiciously round, suspiciously fast, or can't be broken down.

8. How fast do you respond — really?

Test it: send a real question during the sales process and watch the clock. The response speed you get while they're courting you is the best you will ever get. It should be measured in hours, not days.

9. Do you know my kind of group?

A DMC brilliant at incentive travel may be mediocre at coach touring, and vice versa. Ask what share of their work looks like yours — corporate meetings, incentives, leisure groups, tour-operator series — and listen for honesty over salesmanship.

10. What don't you do?

The most trustworthy answer in this industry is a clear "we don't do that". A partner who says "yes" to everything either has no focus or plans to subcontract you to someone you've never vetted. Every good DMC has edges; make them show you theirs.

Red flags worth walking away from

  • No physical presence in the destination — just a sales office somewhere else.
  • Quotes that arrive instantly with no clarifying questions asked. A real proposal requires understanding your brief first.
  • No named contact, or a different person on every email.
  • Vague or missing out-of-hours arrangements.
  • Reluctance to itemise costs or to put cancellation terms in writing.
